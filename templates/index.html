Basic CPR Essential
Size-Up
Before assisting someone who is injured or in distress, it’s crucial to stay calm and take a moment to evaluate the situation. This initial step, known as a “size-up,” ensures your safety and the safety of others before any first aid is provided. In this video, you’ll learn how to quickly assess the environment for potential hazards such as fire, exposed electrical wires, dangerous chemicals, or unstable structures. You’ll also be trained to recognize other risks like moving traffic or violent individuals that could put you or others in danger.

Once you’ve determined that the scene is safe, the video will guide you through the next critical steps: checking if the person is responsive by gently tapping them and speaking to them, calling emergency services for professional help, and deciding when to begin first aid or CPR depending on the person’s condition. These actions are the foundation of effective emergency response and can make a life-saving difference in critical moments.
Basic CPR Beginner
Initial Assessment
Once you’ve ensured the scene is safe, the next crucial step is the initial assessment—a quick but thorough check to determine the person’s condition. This involves checking for signs of life, including normal breathing and a pulse. In this video, you’ll learn how to look for chest movement, listen for breath sounds, and feel for a pulse at key points like the neck or wrist. If the person isn’t breathing or doesn’t have a pulse, you’ll be guided on when and how to begin life-saving measures like CPR or rescue breathing without delay.

If the person is conscious and breathing, the video also explains how to communicate with them clearly and calmly to gather important information. You’ll learn how to ask about pain, medical conditions, and what happened, while also scanning the body for visible injuries like bleeding, swelling, or deformities. This quick but focused assessment helps you prioritize actions and prepare to give the most effective first aid possible until professional help arrives.
Basic CPR Beginner
Head-to-Toe Examination
After the initial assessment, performing a thorough head-to-toe examination is essential to identify any hidden or less obvious injuries. This video guides you step-by-step through how to systematically check each part of the body—starting with the head and moving down through the eyes, neck, chest, arms, abdomen, pelvis, legs, and back. By following a consistent order, you ensure that no area is overlooked, which is especially important when the person may not be able to clearly describe their pain or injuries.

You’ll learn how to look for key signs such as bleeding, bruising, swelling, deformities, or areas that cause pain when touched. The video also emphasizes the importance of clear and gentle communication throughout the examination. Talking to the patient—if they are conscious—helps keep them calm and involved while allowing you to gather valuable information. This detailed check helps you make informed decisions about what first aid may be needed and ensures that emergency responders receive an accurate report when they arrive.
Basic CPR Beginner
Checking Vital Signs
Monitoring vital signs is a key part of understanding a person’s overall condition and how their body is responding to an injury or illness. This video teaches you how to take and interpret basic vital signs, including how to find and count a pulse, measure the breathing rate, and observe the person’s skin for temperature, color, and moisture. Each of these signs provides valuable information about how well the body is functioning and can help identify signs of shock, distress, or internal injuries.

You’ll also learn how to check the pupils for size, equality, and reaction to light—an important indicator of possible head injuries or neurological issues. These vital checks are simple but powerful tools that help you assess the severity of the situation and monitor any changes over time. Knowing how to track these signs allows you to provide better first aid and communicate clearly with emergency responders, giving them the most accurate and helpful information when they take over care.
Basic CPR All Levels
Patient History (SAMPLE)
Collecting a clear and accurate patient history is essential for understanding what may have caused the emergency and how best to treat the person. In this video, you’ll be introduced to the SAMPLE method—a simple and effective way to remember the key questions to ask. SAMPLE stands for: Signs and symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past medical history, Last food or drink, and Events leading up to the emergency. These categories cover the most important information that medical professionals will need when they arrive.

You’ll learn how to ask these questions calmly and respectfully, even in stressful situations, and how to adapt your approach depending on whether the person is conscious, confused, or unresponsive. The video also demonstrates how to record responses clearly, either by writing them down or sharing them verbally with emergency responders. Gathering this information can help doctors and EMTs make faster, more accurate decisions, leading to better care and outcomes for the patient.
Basic CPR Intermediate
Spine Assessment
In situations like falls, vehicle crashes, or sports injuries, there’s a serious risk of spinal injury. This video teaches you how to perform a careful spine assessment to help detect possible damage to the neck or back—without causing further harm. You’ll learn how to recognize warning signs such as neck or back pain, tingling, numbness, or weakness in the arms and legs. These symptoms may indicate a spinal injury, and moving the person unnecessarily could make things worse.

The video emphasizes the importance of keeping the person completely still if a spinal injury is suspected. You’ll learn how to assess their ability to move or feel their limbs by gently asking them to wiggle their fingers or toes—without encouraging any full-body movement. You’ll also be shown how to support the head and neck in a neutral position while waiting for professional responders. Knowing how to manage potential spinal injuries safely can prevent permanent damage and make a crucial difference in a person’s recovery.
Basic CPR Essential
Choking Aid
Choking can become life-threatening quickly. This video teaches how to recognize when someone is choking and how to perform the Heimlich maneuver. You'll learn the proper hand placement and motion, when to call 911, and what to do if the person becomes unconscious and stops breathing.
Basic CPR Essential
CPR Basics
When someone has no pulse and isn’t breathing, their heart has stopped—and every second counts. Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is a life-saving technique that keeps blood and oxygen flowing to the brain and other vital organs until professional help arrives. In this video, you’ll learn how to recognize when CPR is needed and how to perform it with confidence. It begins with high-quality chest compressions—placing your hands in the correct position on the chest, using your body weight for effective depth, and maintaining a steady rhythm of about 100 to 120 compressions per minute.

The video also explains how to give rescue breaths safely, including how to tilt the head back, pinch the nose, and deliver each breath while watching the chest rise. You’ll learn when to alternate between compressions and breaths, and how to adapt the technique if you’re doing hands-only CPR. It also covers when to stop—such as when the person shows signs of life, when a trained responder takes over, or when emergency medical personnel arrive. By mastering CPR, you can give someone their best chance at survival in a critical moment.
Basic CPR Essential
Using an AED
An Automated External Defibrillator (AED) is a powerful tool that can help restart a person’s heart during sudden cardiac arrest. This video walks you through how to use an AED confidently and correctly, even if you’ve never used one before. You’ll learn how to quickly power on the device, expose the person’s chest, and place the pads in the correct positions—typically one pad on the upper right chest and the other on the lower left side. The AED will then analyze the heart’s rhythm and tell you if a shock is needed.

The video also emphasizes the importance of staying calm and listening carefully to the AED’s voice prompts, which guide you step-by-step through the process. You’ll learn how to ensure no one is touching the person during analysis or shock delivery, and how to immediately resume CPR between shocks if advised. When used correctly and in combination with CPR, an AED significantly increases the chances of survival until emergency medical responders arrive and take over.

Emergency Toolkit

Step-by-Step Emergency Response Guide

Learn how to respond confidently in critical moments. This guide covers essential first aid procedures—from CPR and choking to burns, AED use, and more.

CPR
  • Unresponsive & not breathing
  • 100–120 compressions/min
  • 2 inches deep, breaths if trained
  • Gentler for children/infants
CPR can double/triple survival (AHA)
AED
  • Use on non-breathing person
  • Follow voice prompts
  • Attach pads & deliver shock
  • Resume CPR right after
Bleeding
  • Firm pressure with cloth
  • Elevate if possible
  • Tourniquet only if severe
  • Don’t remove embedded objects
Choking
  • Ask if the person is choking
  • Abdominal thrusts above navel
  • Back slaps/chest thrusts for infants
Burns & Cuts
  • Cool with water 10–15 minutes
  • Don’t use ice or ointments
  • Cover deep burns, seek care
Drowning
  • Ensure water safety first
  • Start CPR if not breathing
  • Use AED if available
  • Keep warm on firm surface
Fracture
  • Look for deformity or pain
  • Immobilize with splint
  • Don't move unless necessary
Heatstroke
  • Temp >104°F, confusion
  • Move to shade, cool body
  • Give water if conscious
  • Call emergency services
